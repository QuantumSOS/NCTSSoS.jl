Clifford SAB Explainer

Clifford Symmetry-Adapted Basis
for Pauli SOS Relaxations

Exploit the structure of the Clifford group to block-diagonalize large semidefinite programs in quantum optimization — making intractable problems tractable.

Why this matters: When you optimize over quantum states using SOS (sum-of-squares) relaxations, you build big matrices. Symmetry can shatter them into small, independent blocks — cutting compute time from hours to seconds. The Clifford group is the natural symmetry group of Pauli-algebra problems, but wiring it in is surprisingly subtle.

Quick Overview

NCTSSoS.jl is a Julia library that optimizes noncommutative polynomials via moment/SOS hierarchies — a way to relax hard quantum optimization problems into semidefinite programs (SDPs). This branch adds Clifford symmetry reduction for Pauli-algebra problems, block-diagonalizing the SDP so it's cheaper to solve.

🧩 Pauli Algebra
Quantum observables built from tensor products of I, X, Y, Z matrices — the language of qubit physics.
🔄 Clifford Group
The group of unitaries that permute Pauli operators (up to ±1 signs) under conjugation. Includes H, S, CNOT.
📐 Symmetry-Adapted Basis
A change of basis that splits a big matrix into independent blocks aligned with the group's irreducible representations.
⚡ The Payoff
Instead of one N×N SDP, solve several ni×ni SDPs where Σni² ≪ N². Exponential-feeling speedup.

Core Concepts

1. Pauli Matrices — The Alphabet

Every qubit observable can be written as a linear combination of four 2×2 matrices:

I = ⎡1 0⎤,   X = ⎡0 1⎤,   Y = ⎡0 -i⎤,   Z = ⎡1  0⎤
    ⎣0 1⎦       ⎣1 0⎦       ⎣i  0⎦       ⎣0 -1⎦

For n qubits, a Pauli word is a tensor product like X ⊗ Z ⊗ I — one Pauli per qubit site. There are 4n such words, and they form a basis for all 2ⁿ × 2ⁿ Hermitian matrices.

In NCTSSoS.jl, a Pauli word is stored as a NormalMonomial{PauliAlgebra} — an immutable sequence of per-site labels plus a global phase.

2. SOS / Moment Relaxations — The Optimization Trick

Suppose you want the minimum eigenvalue of a Hamiltonian H (a Hermitian operator written as a sum of Pauli words). Direct diagonalization costs 2n, which blows up fast.

Moment/SOS relaxation replaces this with a semidefinite program (SDP): build a moment matrix Γ whose entries are "expected values" of Pauli words, constrain Γ ⪰ 0 (positive semidefinite), and minimize ⟨H⟩ = Tr(H · Γ). This gives a lower bound on the true ground-state energy, and it often converges as you increase the relaxation order.

The catch: the moment matrix grows polynomially in the number of Pauli words you include — and that number grows exponentially in n. A 10-qubit order-2 moment matrix can be thousands of rows wide. Solving the SDP is O(N³) where N is the matrix dimension.

3. Symmetry-Adapted Basis — Block-Diagonalize Everything

Here's the key insight: if your Hamiltonian H commutes with a group of symmetries G, then the optimal moment matrix respects those symmetries too. By Schur's lemma, you can change basis so the moment matrix becomes block-diagonal — one block per irreducible representation (irrep) of G.

Think of it like this: instead of solving one giant jigsaw puzzle, you sort the pieces by color first. Each color-pile is its own small puzzle, and the total work is far less.

4. The Clifford Group — Pauli's Natural Symmetry

A Clifford gate is a unitary U that maps every Pauli word to another Pauli word (up to ±1 sign) under conjugation:

U · P · U† = ±P'

where P and P' are both Pauli words. The set of all such unitaries forms the Clifford group. The key single-qubit Cliffords are:

The Hadamard gate swaps the X and Z axes, leaving Y (up to sign):

H · X · H† = Z
H · Y · H† = −Y
H · Z · H† = X

Notice the sign flip on Y. Tracking these signs is what makes Clifford SAB harder than plain permutation groups.

The S gate (phase gate) rotates X→Y in the equatorial plane:

S · X · S† = Y
S · Y · S† = −X
S · Z · S† = Z

Z is fixed — S is a rotation about the Z axis by π/2.

The CNOT (controlled-NOT) is a 2-qubit gate. It entangles the action across sites:

CNOT · IX · CNOT† = XX ← site 2 now affects site 1!
CNOT · XI · CNOT† = XI
CNOT · ZI · CNOT† = ZZ
CNOT · IZ · CNOT† = IZ

This is why the existing registry-level SAB can't handle Cliffords: CNOT mixes indices across sites.

The SWAP gate exchanges two qubits:

SWAP · XZ · SWAP† = ZX
SWAP · YI · SWAP† = IY

SWAP is a simple permutation of sites — but it's still a Clifford. Spatial symmetries like lattice reflections are SWAP-like.

The Clifford group is finite (up to global phase) and completely characterized by its action on Pauli words. For n qubits, the Clifford group has order ~2n²+2n — manageable for small n, but you typically work with a small subgroup specified by a handful of generators.

5. The Symplectic Tableau — How to Store a Clifford

A Pauli word on n qubits can be encoded as a binary vector of length 2n: the first n bits track which sites have X or Y (the "X part"), the last n track Z or Y (the "Z part"). Plus a sign bit.

XYZ  →  x-part: [1,1,0], z-part: [0,1,1], sign: +1
(X has x=1,z=0; Y has x=1,z=1; Z has x=0,z=1)

A Clifford gate's conjugation action is a linear map over 𝔽₂ (the field with two elements) on these binary vectors, represented by a 2n × 2n symplectic matrix plus sign corrections. This is the stabilizer tableau representation — O(n²) storage, O(n²) application.

▶ Why "symplectic"?

Two Pauli words either commute or anticommute. The symplectic form ⟨a, b⟩ = aᵀ Ω b mod 2 (where Ω is the standard symplectic matrix) computes this: 0 = commute, 1 = anticommute. Clifford gates must preserve commutation relations, which means their binary representation must be a symplectic matrix — one that satisfies M Ω Mᵀ = Ω over 𝔽₂.

This is the same structure as classical Hamiltonian mechanics, just over a finite field. Not a coincidence — both are about preserving a bilinear form.

6. The Monomial Action — Where the Subtlety Lives

In the existing SAB code for fermionic algebras, symmetries act by signed permutations of registry indices — shuffle the variable labels, maybe flip some signs. That's because fermionic symmetries are essentially site-local.

Clifford symmetries on Pauli words are strictly more general:

  • • Site-mixing: CNOT sends IX → XX. The image involves both sites, not just one. You can't decompose this as "permute site 1, then permute site 2."
  • • Sign flips: Hadamard sends Y → −Y. These signs must propagate correctly through monomials (products of Pauli words at different orders).
  • • Monomial-level action: For an order-d monomial (a product of d Pauli words), the Clifford acts on each word independently, then the signs multiply. The permutation of basis monomials isn't decomposable into per-generator permutations.

This means the integration must wire Clifford actions at the monomial basis level, not the registry level. SymbolicWedderburn handles this via its BySignedPermutations action type — but the group elements must know how to produce the signed permutation on the full monomial list.

Deep Dive

What the Existing SAB Code Does

The file src/optimization/symmetry.jl (1669 lines) implements a symmetry-adapted basis reduction MVP. It currently handles signed permutations of registry indices — the kind of symmetry where each generator shuffles variable labels and maybe flips signs, but each variable maps to exactly one other variable.

The flow: take the basis of monomials in the moment matrix, compute how each group generator permutes them (with signs), feed this to SymbolicWedderburn.symmetry_adapted_basis(), get back block structure + basis-change matrices, and build independent smaller SDPs.

This works for fermionic particle-number symmetries (number-conserving unitaries permute Fock-space basis states by index) but breaks for Clifford symmetries because CNOT-type gates mix indices across sites.

The Gap to Bridge

The core challenge is: SymbolicWedderburn needs to know how each group element permutes (with signs) the monomial basis. For Cliffords:

1

Build the Clifford action on single Pauli words

A Clifford U maps each basis Pauli word P to ±P'. This is a signed permutation on the 4n-element basis. Compute it via the symplectic tableau.

2

Extend to degree-d monomials

A monomial of degree d is a product P₁ · P₂ · … · Pₐ. The Clifford acts on each factor: U(P₁P₂…Pₐ)U† = (UP₁U†)(UP₂U†)…(UPₐU†). Signs multiply. The result may need re-normalization to a canonical monomial.

3

Satisfy the SymbolicWedderburn action interface

Package the signed permutation of monomials in the form SW expects: a GroupsCore.Group with elements that produce signed permutations on the basis.

Pseudocode: Clifford Conjugation

Here's the core computation — how a Clifford element acts on a Pauli word, in tableau form:

# Input: CliffordSymmetry U (tableau: M ∈ F₂^{2n×2n}, signs ∈ {±1}^{4^n})
#        Pauli word P encoded as (x, z) ∈ F₂^{2n}, sign s ∈ {+1,-1}

function conjugate(U::CliffordSymmetry, P::PauliWord)
    # Step 1: Apply the symplectic matrix to the binary vector
    (x′, z′) = U.symplectic_matrix * [P.x; P.z]  # mod 2

    # Step 2: Compute the sign correction
    # This is the tricky part — involves the "phase polynomial"
    # of the tableau, tracking how X/Z generators pick up signs
    sign′ = compute_conjugation_sign(U, P)

    # Step 3: Return the new Pauli word with corrected sign
    return (sign′ * P.sign, PauliWord(x′, z′))
end

# For a degree-d monomial M = P₁ · P₂ · … · Pₐ:
function conjugate(U::CliffordSymmetry, M::PauliMonomial)
    total_sign = 1
    new_factors = []
    for Pᵢ in M.factors
        (sᵢ, Pᵢ′) = conjugate(U, Pᵢ)
        total_sign *= sᵢ
        push!(new_factors, Pᵢ′)
    end
    # Re-canonicalize the product (Pauli algebra rewrite rules)
    return canonicalize(total_sign, new_factors)
end

Worked Example: 2-Qubit Heisenberg + SWAP

The 2-qubit isotropic Heisenberg Hamiltonian is:

H = XX + YY + ZZ

This has SWAP symmetry: swapping the two qubits leaves H invariant. The SWAP group is {I, SWAP} ≅ ℤ₂, with two irreps: the symmetric (trivial) and antisymmetric (sign) representations.

The 16 Pauli words on 2 qubits split under SWAP into:

  • • Symmetric (10 words): II, XX, YY, ZZ, and the 6 symmetric combos like XI+IX, XY+YX, …
  • • Antisymmetric (6 words): the 6 antisymmetric combos like XI−IX, XY−YX, …

The order-1 moment matrix (16×16) block-diagonalizes into a 10×10 and a 6×6 block. That's 100 + 36 = 136 entries instead of 256 — a ~47% reduction. At higher orders and more qubits, the savings compound dramatically.

Common Pitfalls & Gotchas

⚠️ Multi-qubit Cliffords aren't site-local

The existing SAB code assumes each symmetry generator permutes variables independently per site. CNOT breaks this: IX → XX creates cross-site dependence. You must work at the assembled-monomial level, not the per-site registry level. This is the single biggest architectural change.

⚠️ Signs matter and propagate non-trivially

Hadamard sends Y → −Y. For a degree-2 monomial containing two Y factors, the total sign flips twice and cancels. Mess up one sign, and the block decomposition is silently wrong — the SDP will still solve, but give a garbage bound. Test with known analytic results.

⚠️ Group closure can be expensive

The n-qubit Clifford group grows as ~2n²+2n. Even for n=3 that's ~92,000 elements. Only use user-supplied subgroups, and cap the closure computation. Consider storing the Cayley table eagerly for small groups and switching to a Schreier-Sims style approach if performance demands it later.

💡 Canonicalization after conjugation

After applying a Clifford to a Pauli monomial, the result must be re-canonicalized using the Pauli algebra's simplification rules (in src/simplification/pauli.jl). Two Pauli words multiply with a phase: XY = iZ. If your monomial has adjacent factors on the same site, they'll contract.

💡 Dict vs. tableau: start simple

The TASK.md suggests starting with a dict-of-images representation (just store where each Pauli word maps to) and switching to the symplectic tableau only if performance requires it. This is wise — get correctness first. The tableau math is straightforward but sign tracking is finicky to debug.

Test Your Understanding

Q1: Why can't the existing fermionic SAB code handle Clifford symmetries out of the box?

Because the existing code assumes symmetries are signed permutations of registry indices (each variable maps independently to another variable). Multi-qubit Cliffords like CNOT mix variables across sites — IX → XX — which can't be expressed as an index-level permutation. The action must be defined on assembled monomials.

Q2: What does H · Y · H† equal, and why does the sign matter?

H · Y · H† = −Y. The sign matters because it determines how Pauli monomials transform: for a degree-2 monomial YY, the sign flips twice and cancels (giving +YY), while for YX it flips once (giving −YZ). Getting signs wrong silently corrupts the block decomposition.

Q3: Why is the Clifford action on binary vectors a "symplectic" matrix?

Because Clifford gates must preserve the commutation relations between Pauli operators. Two Paulis either commute or anticommute, and this is captured by the symplectic inner product ⟨a,b⟩ = aᵀΩb mod 2. Preserving this form is exactly the definition of a symplectic matrix over 𝔽₂.

Q4: If the 2-qubit SWAP group splits 16 Pauli words into blocks of size 10 and 6, what's the speedup factor for the SDP?

SDP cost scales as O(N³). Without SAB: 16³ = 4096. With SAB: 10³ + 6³ = 1000 + 216 = 1216. That's about a 3.4× speedup. Modest for 2 qubits, but the ratio improves dramatically with more qubits and richer symmetry groups (more irreps = more/smaller blocks).

Q5: Why does the TASK.md recommend starting with a dict-of-images instead of the symplectic tableau?

Because correctness is harder to verify with the tableau approach — the sign computation involves a "phase polynomial" that's easy to get wrong. A dict {PauliWord → (sign, PauliWord)} is trivially inspectable and debuggable. You can validate it against hand computations, then swap in the O(n²) tableau representation later if performance requires it.
