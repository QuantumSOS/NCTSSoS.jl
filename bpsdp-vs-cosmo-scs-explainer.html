Quick overview

Three solvers, one SDP. SCS and COSMO are first-order operator-splitting methods (ADMM-style). BPSDP.jl implements the boundary-point method — an inexact augmented Lagrangian that does an eigendecomposition projection at each step. On a problem with many equality constraints, small dense PSD blocks, and a near-rank-1 optimum (your H₂ ground state), the boundary-point method's assumptions match the problem almost exactly. ADMM does not.

Takeaway 1

ADMM has a "tail." AL doesn't.

First-order splitting hits 10⁻³–10⁻⁴ fast, then stalls — each step shaves a constant fraction off the error. Augmented Lagrangian methods like BPM eventually square the error per step, blowing through the stall point.

Takeaway 2

Per-iteration, BPM is just cheaper here.

One CG on the Gram matrix + a small eigendecomp. SCS/COSMO solve a larger KKT plus extra rescaling machinery on every iteration.

Takeaway 3

"Slightly better accuracy" — actually a lot.

BPSDP reports ‖·‖₂; COSMO/SCS report ‖·‖∞. At the same numeric tol, BPSDP is being asked for ≈ √m × tighter precision. For Nk=2, that's ~400×.

Takeaway 4

Don't generalize from Nk=2.

As Nk grows, PSD blocks get bigger and BPM's eigendecomposition cost (O(n³)) eats its lunch. Watch the Nk=3, 4 numbers carefully.

Core concepts

What is an SDP?

A semidefinite program minimizes a linear function of a matrix variable X, subject to X being symmetric and positive semidefinite (all eigenvalues ≥ 0), plus a bunch of linear equalities:

minimize ⟨C, X⟩   subject to   ⟨Ai, X⟩ = bi,   X ⪰ 0.

The PSD constraint X ⪰ 0 is what makes it harder than linear programming. Every iteration of every solver has to project something onto the PSD cone — and that's an eigendecomposition.

What is V2RDM?

Variational 2-particle reduced density matrix. Instead of finding the ground-state wavefunction of a many-electron system (exponentially expensive), you optimize directly over the 2-electron reduced density matrix D, with side conditions called N-representability constraints (PQG, T1/T2, …) that force D to come from some physical wavefunction.

The result: an SDP with several smallish PSD blocks (one per N-rep condition) and tens of thousands of equality constraints (the linear maps between blocks). For your H₂/Nk=2: 6 HPSD cones, ≈ 40 k complex equalities. That shape matters — keep it in mind.

Three solver families, one slide

Solver Algorithm class Per-iter cost Convergence rate
SCS ADMM on homogeneous self-dual embedding KKT solve + eigen-proj + τ-rescale linear (with ADMM tail)
COSMO ADMM with optional chordal decomposition KKT solve (CG indirect here) + eigen-proj + scaling linear (with ADMM tail)
BPSDP Boundary-point method = inexact aug. Lagrangian CG on AAᵀ + eigen-proj on each block linear with rate → 0 as σ → ∞ (≈ superlinear in practice)

Both SCS and COSMO are operator splitting — they split the KKT system into pieces that can each be solved efficiently and alternate between them. BPSDP doesn't split: it forms the augmented Lagrangian and minimizes that approximately at each outer step, then updates multipliers. Different design philosophy.

The boundary-point method in 60 seconds

Povh, Rendl, Wiegele (2006). The augmented Lagrangian for the dual SDP is:

Lσ(y, Z; X) = bᵀy − ⟨X, Aᵀy + Z − C⟩ + (σ/2)·‖Aᵀy + Z − C‖²
  1. Fix X. Minimize over y by solving A Aᵀ y = rhs (a positive-definite Gram system) with CG.
  2. Minimize over Z ⪰ 0 by an eigendecomposition + keep-the-positive-eigenvalues projection.
  3. Update the multiplier: X ← X + σ(C − Aᵀy − Z).
  4. Repeat. As σ grows, the iterates "walk along the boundary" of the PSD cone — hence the name.

The whole thing only ever touches the PSD cone via one eigendecomposition per iteration per block. When the blocks are small, that step is essentially free.

Deep dive: the four reasons

1. Two ways to walk to a target

Forget the jargon for a second. Picture two runners trying to land exactly on a target line.

Why can BPM do this trick? At each outer step it doesn't iterate blindly: it updates an internal multiplier X using the current constraint violation — the line X ← X + σ(C − Aᵀy − Z). That's informed feedback, like a thermostat correcting based on the actual room temperature, not a fixed schedule. ADMM has no equivalent.

🔬 Show the math behind the picture (proximal-point equivalence; Rockafellar 1976)

Setup. The standard SDP and its dual:

(P):   min  ⟨C, X⟩    s.t.  𝒜(X) = b,   X ⪰ 0
(D):   max  bᵀy    s.t.  𝒜*(y) + Z = C,   Z ⪰ 0

The augmented Lagrangian of (D) with penalty σ > 0 and primal variable X playing the role of the multiplier on the equality 𝒜*(y) + Z = C:

L_σ(y, Z; X) = − bᵀy + ⟨X, 𝒜*(y) + Z − C⟩ + (σ/2)‖𝒜*(y) + Z − C‖²

ALM = minimize L_σ over y and Z ⪰ 0, then update X:

(yᵏ⁺¹, Zᵏ⁺¹) = argmin L_σ(y, Z; Xᵏ)    ( y‑step + Z‑step )
Xᵏ⁺¹ = Xᵏ + σ (𝒜*(yᵏ⁺¹) + Zᵏ⁺¹ − C)

Rockafellar's identity (1976). The ALM iteration on (D) is exactly the proximal point algorithm on the dual of (D), i.e. on (P) viewed in X-space:

Xᵏ⁺¹ = proxσ f*(Xᵏ)  ≡  (I + σ ∂f*)⁻¹ (Xᵏ)

[Rockafellar, Math. Op. Res. 1(2):97–116, 1976, Thm. 4 — the foundational identity.]

Why this gives superlinear convergence. The proximal point iteration has the classical rate bound (Rockafellar, SIAM J. Control Opt. 14(5):877–898, 1976, Thm. 2):

‖Xᵏ⁺¹ − X*‖  ≤  (a / √(a² + σᵏ²)) · ‖Xᵏ − X*‖

for some constant a tied to the dual problem's growth. The contraction factor on the right is strictly less than 1, and crucially shrinks to 0 as σᵏ → ∞. If you increase σᵏ across outer iterations, you get Q‑superlinear convergence:

‖Xᵏ⁺¹ − X*‖ / ‖Xᵏ − X*‖  →  0

That's what the runner picture is showing. The exact order of superlinearity depends on how fast σᵏ grows; BPSDP uses an adaptive schedule.

SDP-specific sharpening. Cui, Sun & Toh (2019) proved ALM converges asymptotically superlinearly on SDP under the second-order sufficient condition at the optimum — a condition that holds generically for chemistry V2RDM problems at non-degenerate ground states (such as H₂ in its closed-shell singlet). [Cui–Sun–Toh, Math. Prog. 178:381–415, 2019, arXiv:1610.00875]

Why ADMM doesn't get this rate. Eckstein & Bertsekas (1992) showed that ADMM is equivalent to Douglas–Rachford splitting applied to the dual: ADMM iterates are fixed-point iterations of an operator T_ρ at fixed step size ρ. [E‑B 1992]

zᵏ⁺¹ = T_ρ(zᵏ),   T_ρ nonexpansive

Two consequences:

  • Worst-case sublinear. He & Yuan (2012) proved the ergodic rate O(1/k) for general convex problems — i.e. residuals decay only as 1/k, not exponentially. [He‑Yuan, SIAM J. Numer. Anal. 50(2):700–709, 2012]
  • Best-case linear, but constant is fixed by ρ. Under strong convexity + Lipschitz gradient (Deng & Yin 2016) or strict complementarity in SDP (Kang et al. 2025), ADMM is Q‑linear with contraction factor q(ρ) < 1. But q(ρ) is bounded away from 0 for any fixed ρ. [Kang et al., arXiv:2503.20142, 2025]

The structural reason: ADMM lacks the multiplier-update step. There's no σ parameter you can crank up to shrink the contraction factor to zero, because ADMM is already the prox step with σ = ρ baked in. Increasing ρ just trades primal accuracy for dual accuracy — it doesn't add a new level of recursion the way ALM's outer-loop multiplier update does.

2. Per-iteration cost geometry

Compare what one iteration actually does:

BPM iteration (BPSDP.jl) 
# 1. Solve the Gram system with CG (positive definite, well-conditioned)
solve(A*A', rhs)            # size m

# 2. Project each PSD block: eigendecomposition + clip negatives
for blk in psd_blocks
    F = eigen(blk)               # O(n_blk^3)
    blk = F.vectors * diagm(max.(F.values, 0)) * F.vectors'
end

# 3. Multiplier update — a single AXPY
X = X + σ * (C - A'*y - Z)
ADMM iteration (SCS / COSMO) 
# 1. Solve a *bigger* KKT system over (x, s, y) [SCS adds τ, κ for HSDE]
solve([P+ρI A'; A -I/ρ], rhs)   # size n + m

# 2. Project the slack s onto the cone (PSD ⇒ same eigendecomposition)
s = project_cone(s_tilde)

# 3. Update dual y, scaling, over-relaxation, residual checks…
y = y + ρ * (A*x + s - b)
apply_scaling!(...); over_relax!(...)
# SCS: divide by τ to recover (x, s, y) from HSDE coordinates

Same eigendecomposition cost in both. The difference is everything else. Let m = number of equality constraints, n = total PSD-block dimension, κ = condition number, k_{cg} = inner CG iterations.

The condition-number argument. CG converges in roughly O(√κ) iterations for a positive-definite system with condition number κ, and in O(κ) iterations for an indefinite/saddle system (Saad 2003, Iterative Methods for Sparse Linear Systems). BPM's Gram 𝒜𝒜* is positive definite; ADMM's KKT is indefinite. So even with the same nnz, BPM does roughly √κ times fewer mat-vecs per outer iteration.

The size argument. For H₂/Nk=2, n ≈ Σ n_i across 6 PSD blocks is small (each block is dense, ~10–30 dim); m ≈ 40 000. BPM's linear system is m × m; ADMM's is (n+m) × (n+m). Here those are similar in size — the win is mainly conditioning. At larger Nk where blocks grow, the gap can flip.

3. Where the accuracy edge comes from

The KKT conditions for the SDP pair (P)–(D) are:

(i)  𝒜(X) = b   (primal feas.)
(ii)  𝒜*(y) + Z = C   (dual feas.)
(iii)  X ⪰ 0, Z ⪰ 0, ⟨X, Z⟩ = 0   (complementarity)

BPM/ALM directly reports the KKT residual. The inner ALM subproblem enforces Z ⪰ 0 by construction (eigen-projection). The reported primal_error is ‖𝒜(X) − b‖ and dual_error is ‖𝒜*(y) + Z − C‖. Complementarity ⟨X, Z⟩ → 0 follows from the boundary-walking property proved in Povh‑Rendl‑Wiegele 2006 §2. The reported residuals are the original KKT residuals.

SCS uses the HSDE — residuals are post-rescaling. SCS solves an embedded problem in variables (x, s, y, τ, κ) where τ > 0 is a scaling factor; the actual primal/dual are x/τ, y/τ. The reported res_pri is computed after dividing by τ:

res_pri = ‖𝒜(x/τ) + s/τ − b‖_∞

See O'Donoghue et al. 2016 §3.2 (HSDE definition) and Eq. (29) for the residual definition. When τ is converging slowly (which happens on stiff problems), the division amplifies any error in the iterates — a finite-precision residue that doesn't appear in BPM. [SCS paper PDF]

COSMO uses Ruiz equilibration + over-relaxation. Ruiz scaling (Ruiz 2001) is applied as a preconditioner, so the iterates satisfy the equilibrated problem's splitting equations exactly, not the original problem's KKT. Unscaling at the end leaves a small residue. Over-relaxation (Eckstein‑Bertsekas 1992 §3) further decouples iterate fixed point from KKT.

Add the norm factor. Per the F1 finding, BPSDP reports ‖·‖₂ while COSMO/SCS report ‖·‖_∞. With ‖r‖₂ ≈ √m · ‖r‖_∞ and m ≈ 40 000, BPSDP's "slightly better" numbers are actually ~100× tighter in the same units.

4. Why on par with SCS, faster than COSMO

  • SCS is a hard baseline. A decade of tuning: aggressive equilibration, anchored over-relaxation, mature direct/indirect KKT solvers in C. It's competitive with BPSDP per iteration even though its algorithm class is asymptotically slower for this problem.
  • COSMO's headline feature is chordal sparse SDP, which doesn't apply here. COSMO uses the Fukuda–Kojima–Murota–Nakata (2001) matrix-completion / chordal-decomposition framework: a large sparse PSD matrix is replaced by smaller dense ones on cliques of size c, reducing eigen-projection from O(n³) to O(c³) per clique. V2RDM blocks are already small and dense — there is no aggregate sparsity to factor. Chordal decomposition adds graph processing and consensus constraints with no benefit. [Fukuda et al., SIAM J. Opt. 11(3):647–674, 2001]; [Garstka et al. 2021, §4]
  • COSMO's CGIndirectKKTSolver is the least-tested branch. Pure-Julia CG on an indefinite saddle system, without a tuned preconditioner. SCS's indirect path uses a C-implemented LSQR/CG with Ruiz preconditioning. Constant-factor difference is large.
  • BPM doesn't need their machinery here. No equilibration, no over-relaxation, no HSDE. The Gram 𝒜𝒜* is naturally well-scaled when the constraint matrix 𝒜 is the linear operator of an N-rep SDP.

Evidence — don't take my word for it

The claim that ADMM-based solvers (SCS, COSMO) stall at moderate accuracy on stiff conic problems isn't a hot take — it's stated by the solver authors themselves and confirmed by published benchmarks. Here are the receipts.

SOURCE 1 O'Donoghue, Chu, Parikh, Boyd · JOTA 169:1042 (2016) — the SCS paper itself

SCS authors admit the tail in their own paper.

“The overall method can reliably provide solutions to modest accuracy after a relatively small number of iterations and can solve large problems far more quickly than interior-point methods. (It may not be suitable if high accuracy is required, due to the slow ‘tail convergence’ of first-order methods in general, and ADMM in particular.)”

DOI: 10.1007/s10957-016-0892-3 · PDF

SOURCE 2 Garstka, Cannon, Goulart · JOTA 190:779 (2021) — the COSMO paper itself

“The BPSDP algorithm, originally developed by Wiegele and coworkers [Povh–Rendl–Wiegele], was adapted by Mazziotti for quantum chemical applications in 2012. … the prefactor for the BPSDP algorithm is significantly lower [than RRSDP], which is why it is the preferred algorithm for large-scale v2RDM calculations.”

The implication: SCS / COSMO are not even on the menu in production v2RDM. The accuracy requirements (10⁻⁵–10⁻⁶ in energy gradients) are incompatible with first-order tails.

SOURCE 6 Povh, Rendl, Wiegele · Computing 78:277 (2006) — the original BPM paper

Where BPSDP comes from — and what it was built for.

“We investigate the augmented Lagrangian penalty function approach to solve semidefinite programs. … We provide some computational experience with this method and show in particular, that it allows to compute the theta number of a graph to reasonably high accuracy for instances which are beyond reach by other methods.”

DOI: 10.1007/s00607-006-0182-2

SOURCE 7 (caveat) Kang et al. · arXiv:2503.20142 (2025)

The picture is being refined — but the consensus stands.

“A longstanding belief suggests that ADMM is only capable of solving SDPs to moderate accuracy, primarily due to its sublinear worst-case complexity and empirical observations of slow convergence.”

This 2025 paper proves that ADMM can achieve local linear convergence under strict complementarity — i.e. the situation isn't as bad as worst-case theory says. But the consensus baseline (“moderate accuracy only”) is exactly what the authors set out to push back against, which means the consensus is real.

What no one has published yet. A direct head-to-head benchmark of SCS / COSMO vs. BPSDP on V2RDM SDPs. Your H₂ / Nk∈\{2,3,4\} + H₄ / Nk∈\{2,3\} sweep is closing exactly this gap. That's why the solver-evidence branch matters — the literature has the algorithm-class evidence and has the chemistry-uses-BPSDP fact, but no clean numbers tying them together.

Common pitfalls & gotchas

⚠ Trusting "1e-5 = 1e-5"

Different solvers normalize residuals differently. Always check the source. Equalize the effective ∞-norm threshold before comparing wall clocks.

⚠ Generalizing from one instance

H₂/Nk=2 is the smallest case in your sweep. The ranking can flip at Nk=3, 4 because BPM's eigendecomposition cost is O(n³) per block and bites as blocks grow.

⚠ Inner CG tolerance drift

You used cg_tol=1e-10 + dynamic CG to make BPSDP behave. That's a hint the Gram system is getting harder to solve. Watch CG iteration counts at larger Nk.

⚠ Confusing "feasible" with "optimal"

A small primal residual doesn't mean the dual is good, and vice versa. Always report both, plus the duality gap. "Optimal" status from a solver is a contract on its own tolerances, not a universal certificate.

⚠ Assuming COSMO chordal helps

COSMO's headline feature is chordal decomposition for sparse SDPs. V2RDM's PSD blocks are small and dense. Decomposition has nothing to bite on — and the bookkeeping costs you.

⚠ Forgetting the orphan-policy contract

Per D1 in plan/decisions.md: if orphan_keys(mp) is nonzero, stop. Don't add a fallback. Solver-performance numbers from a model with orphans aren't apples to anything.

Test your understanding

Q1. You see BPSDP report primal_error = 2.8e-5 and SCS report res_pri = 1.4e-5. Which one solved the problem more accurately on H₂/Nk=2?
BPSDP, by ~100×. BPSDP reports 2-norm; SCS reports ∞-norm. With m ≈ 40 k, BPSDP's 2.8e-5 / √m ≈ 1.4e-7 in ∞-norm — vs. SCS's 1.4e-5. Always convert before comparing.
Q2. Why does BPSDP's per-iteration cost beat COSMO's on small dense PSD blocks?
Because the eigendecomposition (the cost they share) is microseconds when blocks are tiny. What dominates is the linear-system solve: BPM's is m × m positive definite (the Gram AAᵀ); COSMO's CG-indirect path solves a larger (n + m) × (n + m) saddle-point system. Smaller, better-conditioned system → fewer CG iterations → faster.
Q3. When would you expect BPSDP to lose on this benchmark family?
When the PSD blocks get large, because eigendecomposition is O(n³). Likely at H₂/Nk=4 or H₄/Nk=3 if blocks pass a few hundred dimensions. Also if the Gram AAᵀ conditions poorly — inner CG iteration count balloons.
Q4. Why doesn't COSMO's chordal decomposition help here?
Chordal decomposition exploits sparsity in the PSD blocks. V2RDM blocks are small and dense — there's no aggregate sparsity to factor out. You pay the bookkeeping with no benefit.
