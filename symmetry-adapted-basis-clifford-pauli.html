SAB × Clifford-Pauli

NCTSSoS.jl · symmetry reduction

Symmetry-Adapted Basis,
plus Clifford-Pauli symmetry

How to take one big positive-semidefinite block in a moment relaxation and, using the problem's symmetry, slice it into several small independent blocks — the same optimal value, a dramatically cheaper SDP. And how the new code lets a Clifford group supply that symmetry for Pauli (qubit) problems.

Why this matters: An SDP's cost explodes with the size of its PSD block. If your objective and constraints don't change under some symmetry, you are solving the same little problem many times over. Block-diagonalization removes that redundancy exactly — no approximation, just less arithmetic.

Audience note: this assumes you already know polynomial optimization (SOS, moment matrices, the PSD constraint, the relaxation hierarchy). It assumes no abstract algebra — every group-theory term is a dashed-underlined chip you can click for a one-line plain-English definition.

Quick overview

You already build a moment matrix indexed by a basis of monomials and demand it be positive semidefinite. The symmetry-adapted basis (SAB) is a change of coordinates on that basis, chosen so the moment matrix becomes block-diagonal. You then solve each block on its own. The Clifford-Pauli piece is simply where the symmetry comes from when your variables are Pauli operators: a Clifford gate, acting by conjugation, shuffles Pauli words and maybe flips a sign — a signed permutation of your basis, which is exactly what the reduction machinery needs.

1 · The lever

If a finite group leaves the objective and all constraints unchanged, the moment matrix is forced to commute with that group. Commuting matrices share a block structure.

2 · The payoff

One n×n PSD constraint becomes several smaller ones. Example below: a 7×7 block splits cleanly into 4×4 and 3×3.

3 · The Pauli twist

For qubit problems, Clifford gates (H, S, CNOT, SWAP) conjugate Paulis to ±Paulis. That ± sign is why a plain permutation isn't enough — you need signed permutations.

4 · The discipline

The code is deliberately strict: it errors if the symmetry doesn't actually preserve the problem, if the basis isn't closed, or if the gate isn't a genuine Clifford. Wrong-but-quiet is the enemy.

Core concepts, simplest first

1 · The bottleneck you already know

Your moment relaxation picks a basis of monomials \(Q = (q_1, \dots, q_n)\) and builds the moment matrix \(M_{ij} = \langle q_i^\dagger q_j\rangle\), then demands \(M \succeq 0\). The interior-point solver's work scales steeply with \(n\) — roughly like \(n^3\) per iteration just for the linear algebra, worse once you count the number of moment variables. Halving \(n\) is not a 2× win; it can be a 5–10× win. So the whole game is: make the PSD block smaller without changing the answer.

2 · A symmetry of the problem

Suppose there's an operation \(g\) — say "swap qubit 1 and qubit 2" — that rearranges your variables but leaves the objective and every constraint invariant. Collect all such operations and you get a group \(G\). The way each \(g \in G\) shuffles your monomials is a group action.

Group the monomials by what the action does to them: an orbit is the full set of monomials reachable from one starting monomial by applying every \(g\). The punchline you can feel without any algebra: monomials in the same orbit must have the same expectation value (up to a sign the action might introduce). So they should share a single moment variable instead of getting one each. That alone shrinks the variable count.

3 · Why symmetry forces a block structure

Here is the one genuinely non-elementary idea, told without machinery. Write each operation \(g\) as a matrix \(\rho(g)\) that permutes (and sign-flips) the basis — that's a representation. Because \(g\) preserves the problem, the moment matrix \(M\) commutes with every \(\rho(g)\): \(\rho(g)\,M = M\,\rho(g)\). The set of all matrices that commute with the whole group is called the commutant, and \(M\) is trapped inside it.

Now the key fact (Schur's lemma): you can re-coordinate the space into indivisible pieces — irreducible representations — and a matrix that commutes with the group cannot mix different pieces. In the right coordinates, \(M\) is block-diagonal: zero between pieces, free inside each piece. Finding that coordinate change algorithmically is the Wedderburn decomposition, and the library SymbolicWedderburn.jl does exactly this.

The intuition in one line: symmetry says "these directions are interchangeable," and a matrix that respects interchangeable directions can't couple them — so it falls apart into blocks.

4 · The symmetry-adapted basis itself

The symmetry-adapted basis is that coordinate change. Instead of indexing \(M\) by raw monomials \(q_1,\dots,q_n\), you index it by carefully chosen combinations of monomials, sorted so that everything transforming the same way sits together (each such group is an isotypic component, and it becomes one block). Same matrix, same eigenvalues, same optimum — just written in coordinates where the block structure is visible and the solver can attack each block alone.

5 · The Pauli alphabet

Now specialize to qubit problems. The variables are Pauli operators \(X, Y, Z\) sitting on sites (qubits). A monomial here is a Pauli word like \(X_1 Z_2\). The algebra is simple and rigid:

  • • Each squares to identity: \(X^2 = Y^2 = Z^2 = I\).
  • • Different sites commute: \(X_1\) and \(Z_2\) don't interfere.
  • • Same site is cyclic: \(XY = iZ,\; YZ = iX,\; ZX = iY\) (and reversed gives \(-i\)).

That \(i\) is a phase. It's why multiplying Pauli words sometimes spits out factors of \(\pm i\) that must be tracked and, for a valid symmetry, must collapse to a real \(\pm 1\).

6 · The Clifford group: Pauli's natural symmetry

Where do symmetries of a Pauli problem come from? The Clifford group. A Clifford operation \(U\) has one defining magic property: when you conjugate a Pauli word — sandwich it as \(U P U^{-1}\) — you always get back another Pauli word, times \(\pm 1\). It never produces a messy sum. (Formally, the Clifford group is the normalizer of the Pauli group.)

Read that again with the earlier idea in mind: conjugation by a Clifford maps each basis monomial to one other basis monomial with a sign. That is precisely a signed permutation of the basis — the exact input the Wedderburn machinery wants. So Clifford gates are the bridge: they are the symmetries that keep your Pauli basis closed, with only a harmless \(\pm\) bookkeeping.

Deep dive: how the code does it

The seam: a transformation, not a rewrite

The design choice that keeps this sane: symmetry is a pass that consumes the already-built symbolic relaxation and emits a smaller, ordinary moment problem. The solver, the dualizer, the sparsity code — all untouched. When you set SolverConfig(symmetry = …), the build routes through moment_relax_symmetric(…) instead of the plain path, and everything downstream still sees a normal MomentProblem — it just has several small PSD blocks instead of one big one.

What moment_relax_symmetric actually does

  1. 1Enumerate the group from your generators (e.g. CliffordSymmetry(:SWAP, 1, 2)) into a finite CliffordSymmetryGroup.
  2. 2Check invariance (strict). Every group element must leave the objective and all constraints unchanged, or it throws. No silent wrongness.
  3. 3Check closure (strict). Acting on any basis monomial must land back inside the same basis's span, or it throws.
  4. 4Decompose the half-basis with SymbolicWedderburn.symmetry_adapted_basis, using the Pauli-Clifford action as a BySignedPermutations action.
  5. 5Block-diagonalize the symbolic PSD matrix into the adapted blocks.
  6. 6Orbit-reduce the moment variables: monomials in one orbit collapse to a single representative (carrying its ± sign).
  7. 7Emit a smaller MomentProblem and a SymmetryReport (group order, block sizes, invariant-moment count).

Worked example: 2-qubit Heisenberg + SWAP

This is a real regression test in the repo. The Heisenberg interaction \(H = \tfrac14(X_1X_2 + Y_1Y_2 + Z_1Z_2)\) is obviously unchanged if you swap the two qubits — that's the symmetry. Order-1 basis: \(\{I, X_1, X_2, Y_1, Y_2, Z_1, Z_2\}\), so one dense \(7\times7\) block.

Julia — enabling Clifford symmetry 
# 2-qubit Heisenberg interaction over the Pauli algebra
reg, (σx, σy, σz) = create_pauli_variables(1:2)
H     = sum((1/4) * op[1] * op[2] for op in (σx, σy, σz))   # ¼(X₁X₂+Y₁Y₂+Z₁Z₂)
pop   = polyopt(H, reg)
basis = [one(σx[1]); σx; σy; σz]                       # I, X₁, X₂, Y₁, Y₂, Z₁, Z₂  (7 words)

cfg = SolverConfig(
    moment_basis = basis,
    cs_algo      = NoElimination(),                    # dense: one PSD block
    ts_algo      = NoElimination(),
    symmetry     = SymmetrySpec(CliffordSymmetry(:SWAP, 1, 2)),  # swap the two qubits
)

_, report = moment_relax_symmetric(pop, sparsity.corr_sparsity,
                                   sparsity.cliques_term_sparsities, cfg.symmetry)

report.group_order      # 2
report.psd_block_sizes  # [4, 3]  ← the 7×7 block split into a 4×4 and a 3×3

Why \([4,3]\)? SWAP pairs each site-1 Pauli with its site-2 twin and fixes \(I\). The orbits are \(\{I\},\{X_1,X_2\},\{Y_1,Y_2\},\{Z_1,Z_2\}\). The symmetric combinations \(I,\,X_1{+}X_2,\,Y_1{+}Y_2,\,Z_1{+}Z_2\) form a \(4\times4\) block; the antisymmetric ones \(X_1{-}X_2,\,Y_1{-}Y_2,\,Z_1{-}Z_2\) form a \(3\times3\) block. Off-block entries are forced to zero. The reported block_provenance is :wedderburn for both.

The heart: conjugation as a signed-permutation action

A CliffordSymmetry stores the action as signed images of single Pauli letters, e.g. σx[1] => (+1, σz[1]). The named constructors fill these in for you: :H gives \(X\!\to\!Z,\,Y\!\to\!-Y,\,Z\!\to\!X\); :S gives \(X\!\to\!Y,\,Y\!\to\!-X,\,Z\!\to\!Z\); and so on. To act on a whole word, you map each letter, multiply the images, re-simplify to Pauli normal form, and accumulate the sign:

Julia — the monomial action (paraphrased from symmetry.jl) 
function _act_monomial(g::CliffordSymmetry, mono::NormalMonomial{PauliAlgebra})
    sign = 1
    word = Int[]
    for idx in mono.word
        letter_sign, image = _clifford_letter_image(g, idx)  # σ ↦ (±1, σ′)
        sign *= letter_sign
        append!(word, image.word)
    end
    image_word, phase = simplify(PauliAlgebra, word)        # XY→iZ, σ²→I, sort sites
    sign *= _phase_to_clifford_sign(phase)                 # ±i ⇒ error; +1/−1 ⇒ keep
    return sign, NormalMonomial{PauliAlgebra}(image_word)
end

The _phase_to_clifford_sign guard is the safety rail: a genuine Clifford conjugation of a Hermitian Pauli word can only produce a real \(\pm 1\). If multiplication leaves a leftover \(\pm i\), the supplied images were not a valid Clifford and the code errors instead of fabricating a nonsensical "symmetry."

A pretty subtlety: orbits that must be zero

Because the permutation is signed, walking around an orbit can return you to where you started but with a flipped sign. If a monomial \(m\) maps back to itself with sign \(-1\), then \(\langle m\rangle = -\langle m\rangle\), which forces \(\langle m\rangle = 0\). The orbit reducer detects exactly this: when the signs around an orbit are inconsistent, it marks the whole orbit's moment variable as identically zero rather than merging it. That's not a bug to work around — it's a free constraint the symmetry hands you.

Common pitfalls & gotchas

The symmetry doesn't actually preserve the problem

If your group changes the objective or a constraint, the reduction is silently wrong. The code defends you: check_invariance is on by default and throws. Don't disable it unless you've proven invariance by hand.

The basis isn't closed under the action

Block-diagonalization only works if acting on a basis monomial lands back in the basis's span. If it escapes, you'd be projecting onto the wrong space. The closure check throws with the offending image.

Forgetting the sign (plain vs. signed permutation)

Clifford conjugation gives \(\pm\) Pauli words. Treating it as an unsigned permutation merges orbits that should cancel, and you lose the "this moment is zero" constraints. The action must be a signed permutation.

Supplying images that aren't a real Clifford

Custom image dictionaries are validated: they must preserve commutation/anticommutation between letters and same-site Pauli multiplication. Garbage in → ArgumentError, not garbage out. Sources must also be single Pauli letters.

Expecting it to compose with everything (yet)

This slice is deliberately dense and strict: one clique, no term sparsity, multiplicity-free blocks. Repeated irreps, multi-clique composition, and term-sparsity interplay are explicitly out of scope and will error rather than guess.
